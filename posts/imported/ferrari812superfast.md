# Jeremy Gallery - Ferrari 812 Superfast

![Jeremy Gallery - Ferrari 812 Superfast](https://px-web-images-v2.pixpa.com/dYPALdO5qymANyUrkn5xMRh-bnJYOKtYP2ZLao-oA_U/rs:fit:1200:0/q:80/czM6Ly9waXhwYS10ZXN0L2NvbS9sYXJnZS82OTA1MjcvNjkwNTI3LTE2ODE4MjgyODAtMDAwLTgxMi1zdXBlci0wMDUuanBn)

Jun 10, 2019

Ferrari 812 Superfast

When I first began photographing cars, one lesson revealed itself almost immediately:

In tight spaces—garages, showrooms, alleys—you can try an iPhone’s 27mm, a Canon 24mm, or anything in between, but sooner or later the wide-angle lens becomes inevitable. It’s the only way to breathe inside these narrow rooms, the only way to let the car fully exist in the frame.

On this day, a Ferrari 812 Superfast rested quietly in a private showroom—one of the very earliest imports of the model. Under soft light, the V12 silhouette looked almost sculptural, a piece of machinery holding its breath.

Here, the choice was clear: 24mm f/2.8—nothing else would do.

You can reach for a 35mm or 50mm to pull the car closer, to isolate the forged rims, the carbon details, the sharp edge of a headlamp. Those focal lengths speak in whispers.

But a 24mm tells the whole story—car and environment woven together, a scene rather than a portrait. The space wraps around the machine, and the machine answers back.

So I stayed and kept looking.

Because some cars invite you to linger, and the 812 Superfast—with its presence, its proportions, its quiet confidence—is one you don’t forget easily.
